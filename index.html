Rethinking the github project page

a design exercise from an aspiring githubber september 2012

Hello, Github.

My name is Ale Muñoz. I am a UX designer from Spain, and I’ve been wishing to work for Github since I was aware one could, you know, work *for* Github.

Introduction Hey ho, let’s go

Whenever I start a design project, I try to look for insights I can use as a starting point. If time and budget permits it, I like to research a bit about the problem at hand, usually by speaking with or simply observing current or potential users.

For this exercise, I’ve created some assumptions based on my experience when talking with non-technical users about Github and based my insights (and work) on these assumptions, even if some of them are obviously not perfect.

Step 1: starting point analysis

What’s wrong with the current project page, imho

Whenever I visit a project page on github.com (something that happens quite frequently, I must say) the first thing I think is:

Man, you’ve surely gained a lot of features through the years… me

My designer colleagues are not usually that kind. The most repeated comment is that the page lacks hierarchy, looks too busy and doesn’t really drive your attention to any particular point. Also, it’s not clear at all what the site is about.

What is this, exactly? anonymous designer

In all honesty, I love Github. I love the features, I love the community, I love the workflow. One of my main frustrations with it is that I cannot use it more to collaborate with my team. Whenever I try to convince some non-technical person to join this amazing experience, I’m greeted with blank stares or suspicious looks.

But this... this is for programmers! I’m not a programmer. my wife

So, even if some efforts have been made by journalists to understand and use Github, I think there’s still some room for improvement here.

So, to recap, here’s the list of insights I’ll work with for this (quick & dirty) redesign:

— Page looks too flat, lacks hierarchy.

— There are too many visible options. This hinders understanding and makes the workflow hard to understand for non-hardcore profiles.

— There is no easy to reach information for non-technical users, where they can learn what the project is about.

— Visually, the page looks too busy. Boxes and lines could be substituted by white space.

Step 2: sketching

Thinking with a pencil (or stylus)

I truly believe sketching is what distinguishes a designer from any other professional. Some scholars even suggest that the conversations we have with our sketches are a different kind of language:

The way designers work may be inexplicable, not for some romantic or mystical reason, but simply because these processes lie outside the bounds of verbal discourse: they are literally indescribable in linguistic terms. J. Daley, “Design Creativity and The Understanding of Objects”, Design Studies, Vol. 3, No 3

So I started a conversation with my ideas, refining them and finally settling on some principles for the design:

— progressive disclosure
— whitespace
— a home for non-techies
— clear calls to action

And with these I ran to my new found love (Sketch.app) and started working with grids, and shapes, and colors...

About the author

I am Github user #3832, bomberstudios.

My current job position says I’m a Design Director at Europe’s largest strategic design consultancy, but I like to call myself a “design hacker”. Thus, I’m looking for a new gig that better fits my philosophical approach to design: thinking, designing and actually building the damn thing.

Don’t get me wrong: analyzing, researching, defining and conceptualizing products and services is *cool*, but I’m one of those designers that will roll up his sleeves and build a working prototype just to explain how fast an animation should move (after all, *speed* is a design decision). Implementation details are too important to be left out to chance.

I like reading, photography, and on my (very scarce) free time I like to develop extensions for my favorite design tool, Fireworks and come up with ways of doing more with less (usually involving bits of Ruby).

I’ve been known to contribute time (and code) to various open source projects, beta programs and local groups, and I'm a frequent speaker on local design-related events. I’ve also taught design at some master degrees in Madrid.

When I retire, I plan on designing and coding the definitive wiki system. It will feature proper transclusion, an interface my mother will understand, and beautiful typography.
